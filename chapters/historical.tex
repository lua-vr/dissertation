\chapter{Historical behavior}\label{cha:historical-behavior}

Ergodic theory tries to find order in chaos by showing that even if a dynamical system looks complicated, the statistical properties of its orbits are often well behaved. But what if the statistics don't even exist for most orbits? When this happens for all orbits starting in a set of positive reference measure, the phenomenon is known as \emph{non-statistical}, or \emph{historical} behavior.

More precisely, by existence of statistics, we mean the weak convergence of the empirical measures \(\Emp[n]{x} \weakto \mu\) to an asymptotic measure \(\mu\). To say that \((\Emp[n]{x})_n\) does not converge, therefore, is equivalent (in the compact setting) to the existence of a continuous observable \(\varphi \colon X \to \RR\) such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:3}
  \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i = 0}^{n - 1} \varphi(f^i(x))
\end{equation}
does not exist. (The fact that we don't need to quantify over measures that are possible limits here is a consequence of \cref{cor:conv_of_exists}.) We then say that a diffeomorphism \(f\) of a compact manifold presents historical behavior if \(\Emp[n]{x}[spar, n]\) does not converge for \(x\) in a set of positive Lebesgue measure.

\section{History}
The ``historical'' terminology was introduced by Ruelle in~\cite{ruelleHistoricalBehaviourSmooth2001}, suggesting that for \cref{eq:3} not to converge, the sequence must keep ``changing its mind'' as \(n\) goes to infinity, and therefore has some sort of history. For instance, in the absence of external forces like human activity, we could expect that general patterns in climate would follow some predictable statistics, while with climate change we see that such statistics are not consistent along history. In the article, Ruelle recognized the problem of \textit{nonrecurrent} behavior of orbits as going against the expectation that recurrent behavior is generic in the space of diffeomorphisms, and presented the open problem of finding whether historical behavior can exist in a persistent manner, making some physical justifications for this possibility. He cites the theory of spin glasses, where the time evolution of the system is akin to a random walk in a random potential: a particle stays arbitrarily large amounts of time exploring some valley in the potential before eventually crossing the barrier to another valley, in such a way that time averages like \cref{eq:3} do not exist. As we shall see in this chapter, we can indeed produce historical behavior by constructing a diffeomorphism whose orbits behave like random walks, albeit not necessarily persistently.

Later, Floris Takens exposed the problem again in~\cite{takensOrbitsHistoricBehaviour2008}, giving some promising examples that were known at the time and also making some remarks about generiticity of points without asymptotic measures. For this reason and his work in related matters, the open question also became known as \textit{Taken's Last Problem}.

\begin{example}[Logistic family]
   The family of interval maps \(f_a(x) = ax(1 - x)\) for \(0 < a \le 4\) is known as the logistic family, and is one of the earliest examples where historical behavior is present ``abundantly'': in~\cite{hofbauerQuadraticMapsAsymptotic1990}, it is shown that for an uncountable number of parameters \(a\), Lebesgue almost every orbit in \([0,1]\) has no asymptotic measure. The example is not persistent, however.
\end{example}

  Another non-persistent example of historical behavior was given by Bowen (see~\cite{takensHeteroclinicAttractorsTime1994} for the attribution and a more formal description). The ``problem'' with this example is that the saddle connections are not stable, as shown in the second picture.
\end{example}

\section{Historical behavior for skew products}

\NewObject\Fun\Mu{\mu}

Let \((X,\mu)\) be a Borel probability space, where \(X\) is compact and metrizable, and \(F \colon X \times I \to X \times I\) a skew product of the form
\begin{equation*}
  F(x,y) = (g(x),f_x(y))
\end{equation*}
where \(g \colon X \to X\) is ergodic and measure-preserving. Suppose there is a homeomorphism \(\gamma \colon I \to \RR[closure]\) and a measurable function \(p \colon X \to \RR\) such that, if we set \(t_x(y) = y + p(x)\), we have \[t_{x} \circ \gamma = \gamma \circ f_x\] for all \(x \in X\), where \(t_x\) is to be interpreted as the translation by \(p(x)\). 
\[
  \begin{tikzcd}
    I \arrow[d, "\gamma"]\arrow[r, "f_{x}"] & I \arrow[d, "\gamma"]\\
    \RR[closure] \arrow[r,"t_{x}"] & \RR[closure]
  \end{tikzcd}
\]
Since the \(t_x\) are translations, there is no loss of generality in assuming \(\gamma(0) = -\infty\), \(\gamma(1) = +\infty\) and \(\gamma\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = 0\), so we shall adopt this convention for simplicity.

\begin{example}[Zero Schwarzian]
  In the example of~\citeauthor{bonifantSchwarzianDerivativesCylinder2008} from \cref{cha:bonifant-milnor}, if all the fiber maps \(f_x\) satisfy \(\SchD f_x \equiv 0\), then the condition above is satisfied. Indeed, by the last item of \cref{prop:schwarzian}, each \(f_x\) is a fractional linear transformation. Therefore, if we define \(\gamma(y) = \log y - \log (1 - y)\), for \(f_x(y) = \frac{a_x y}{1 + (a_x - 1)y}\) and \(p(x) = \log a_x\), we have
  \begin{align*}
    \gamma(f_x(y)) &= \log\left(\frac{a_x y}{1 + (a_x - 1)y} \right) - \log\left(1 - \frac{a_x y}{1 + (a_x - 1)y}  \right)\\
    &= \log\left(\frac{a_x y}{1 - y} \right) = \log a_x + \log \frac{y}{1 - y} = t_x(\gamma(y)).
  \end{align*}

  In this specific case, we have \(f_x'(0) = - f_x'(1) = a_x\). Therefore, \[\Lyap{\mathcal{A}_0} = - \Lyap{\mathcal{A}_1} = \int p \;d\mu.\]
  By the pointwise ergodic theorem,
  \begin{equation*}
    \frac{\gamma(f_x^n(y)) - \gamma(y)}{n} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i = 0}^{n - 1} p(g^n(x)) \to \int p \;d\mu.
  \end{equation*}
  Therefore, if \(\int p \;d\mu \neq 0\), then either \(\gamma(f_x^n(y)) \to \infty\) or \(\gamma(f_x^n(y)) \to - \infty\), so all orbits in \(X \times (0,1)\) converge to one of the boundaries, which one depending only on the sign of \(\Lyap{\mathcal{A}_0}\).
  
  The interesting case, therefore, is when \(\int p \;d\mu = 0\). In the article, it is shown that this case displays historical behavior, but only for the very particular case of \(g = kx \pmod 1\) and \(p\) being constant on each branch \(\interval[open right]{i/k}{(i + 1)/k}\). It is then conjectured that historical behavior would still be present for the same \(g\) but a continuous \(p \colon X \to \RR\) with zero mean, in general. For the rest of the section, we explain how results already present in the literature, namely weak or almost sure invariance principles, answer their question in the affirmative.
\end{example}

At this point, we recommend that the reader has familiarized themselves with \cref{sec:brownian-motion}, but we can briefly remind ourselves of some notions defined in there. Let \(\xi = (p \circ g^{i})_{i \geq 0}\). We can see \(\xi\) as a sequence of random variables defined on the probability space \((X,\mu)\). Accordingly, we have their partial sums \(\PSum[n,rv=\xi] = \sum_{i = 1}^n\xi_i\) and the sequence of processes \(\DProc[n,rv=\xi] \colon \Omega \to \WPS\) given by
\[\DProc[n,rv=\xi]{\omega}{t} = \frac{\PSum[\floor{nt},rv=\xi]{\omega}}{\sqrt{n}},\]
as explained in that section. We recall that \(W \in \Meas[1]{\CWPS}\) is the Wiener measure, and that we say \(\xi\) satisfies an \emph{weak invariance principle} the processes \(\DProc[n,rv = \xi]\) converge (weakly) in distribution to \(\sigma W\) for some \(\sigma > 0\). The theorem below shows that if we assume this condition for \(g\) and \(p\), we have historical behavior.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:wip-historical}
  If the weak invariance principle is valid for the pair \((g, p)\), in the sense \(\DProc[n,rv = \xi,spar,pf]\mu \weakto \sigma W\) for \(\sigma > 0\) and \(\xi = (p \circ g^{i})_{i \geq 0}\), then \(F\) has historical behavior. More specifically,
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:emp-closure}
    \set{\delta_-, \delta_+} \subseteq \set{e_n(x,y)}[n \in \NN, ']
  \end{equation}
  where \(\delta_- = \mu \otimes \delta_{0}\) and \(\delta_+ = \mu \otimes \delta_{1}\) and the limit points are taken in the weak-\(\ast\) topology.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
  Let \(\delta > 0\) and \(s_{\delta} \colon [0,1] \to \RR\) be any continuous function such that \(s_{\delta}(0) = 0\) and \(s_{\delta}(t) > 1 + \delta\) for \(t > \delta\).
  Let \(U_{\delta} = B_{\delta}(s_{\delta})\) be an open ball of radius \(\delta\) around \(s_{\delta}\) in the uniform topology. By \cref{thm:forgery}, we know \(W(U_{\delta}) > 0\), so by \cref{thm:portmanteau}
  \begin{equation*}
    \liminf_{n \to \infty}(\DProc[n,spar,pf]\mu)(U_{\delta}) \geq \sigma W(U_{\delta}) > 0.
  \end{equation*}
  Therefore,
  \begin{equation*}
    S_{\delta} = \limsup_{n \to \infty}\, \DProc[n,spar,inv]{U_{\delta}} \subseteq X
  \end{equation*}
  has positive measure. This set is also invariant for \(g\) because
  \begin{align*}
    g^{-1}(S_{\delta})
    &= \limsup_{n \to \infty} \set{x \in X \where \DProc[n]{g(x)} \in U_{\delta}}\\
    &= \set[par = \Bigg]{x \in X : \liminf_{n \to \infty} \sup_t\abs*{\frac{\PSum[\floor{nt} + 1]{x} - p(x)}{\sqrt{n}} - s_{\delta}(t)} <\delta }\\
    &= \set[par = \Bigg]{x \in X : \liminf_{n \to \infty} \sup_t\abs*{\frac{\PSum[\floor{nt'}]{x}}{\sqrt{n}} - s_{\delta}(t)} <\delta }  \text{ where }t' = t + 1/n\\
    &= \set[par = \Bigg]{x \in X : \liminf_{n \to \infty} \sup_t\abs*{\frac{\PSum[\floor{nt}]{x}}{\sqrt{n}} - s_{\delta}(t)} <\delta } = S_{\delta}.
  \end{align*}
  Therefore \(\mu(S_{\delta}) = 1\). Now, notice that if \(x \in S_{\delta}\), then there is \(n_k^+ \to \infty\) for which for every \(k\), \(D_{n_k}^{\xi}(x) \in U_{\delta}\). This in turn implies that
  \begin{align*}
    \#\set[par = auto]{ 0 \leq i < n_k^+ \where S_{i}^{\xi}(x) > \sqrt{\textstyle n^+_k} } \geq (1 - \delta)n_k^{+}.
  \end{align*}
  Analogously, by repeating the argument with \(- s_{\delta}\), we find \(n_k^- \to \infty\) such that for every \(k\),
  \begin{align*}
    \#\set[par = auto]{ 0 \leq i < n_k^- \where S_{i}^{\xi}(x) < -\sqrt{\textstyle n^-_k} } \geq (1 - \delta)n_k^{-}.
  \end{align*}
  By considering \(E = \bigcap_{i = 1}^{\infty} U_{1/i} \subseteq X\), a set of full measure, and applying a diagonalization argument to the previous two observations, for each \(x \in E\) we find \(n_k^+, n_k^- \to \infty\) for which
  \begin{equation*}
    \frac{1}{n_k^+}\#\set[par = auto]{ 0 \leq i < n_k^+ \where S_{i}^{\xi}(x) > \sqrt{\textstyle n^+_k} } \to 1
  \end{equation*}
  and
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:6}
    \frac{1}{n_k^-}\#\set[par = auto]{ 0 \leq i < n_k^- \where S_{i}^{\xi}(x) < -\sqrt{\textstyle n^-_k} } \to 1.
  \end{equation}

  Now, by ergodicity of \(g\) and \cref{lem:erg-iff-basin-full-meas}, let \(S = E \cap \Basin{\mu}\) be a full measure set where
  \begin{equation*}
    \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i = 0}^{n - 1}\varphi(g^i(x)) \to \int_X \varphi(z) \;d\mu(z)
  \end{equation*}
  for all \(\varphi \in \Cs{X}\) and \(x \in S\).
  It suffices to show that for all \(x \in S\),
  \[e_{n^-_k}(x,y) \weakto \delta_- \quad\text{ and }\quad e_{n^+_k}(x,y) \weakto \delta_+.\]
  We only show the first convergence, since the second is analogous.

  Let \(\phi \in \Cs{X \times I}\) and \(\varepsilon > 0\). Since \(\phi\) is uniformly continuous, there is some \(k_1\) for which
  \begin{equation*}
    \sup\set[par = \big]{\abs{\phi(x,0) - \phi(x, s)} : x \in X, s \in [0,1/k_1]} < \varepsilon.
  \end{equation*}
  Let \(D = \set{i \where f^{(i)}_x(y) < 1/k_1}\), and let \(N\) be big enough so that for \(k \geq N\),
  \begin{equation*}
    \abs[\Bigg]{\frac{1}{n_k^-} \sum_{i = 0}^{n_k^- - 1}\phi(F^i(x,0)) - \int_X \phi(z,0) \;d\mu(z)} < \varepsilon,
  \end{equation*}
  which is possible since \(x \in \sS\) and \(\varphi(x) = \phi(x,0)\) is continuous, and also big enough so that
  \[\frac{1}{n_k^-}\# \set{ 0 \leq i < n_k^- \where i \notin D } < \varepsilon / \lVert \phi \rVert_{\infty},\]
  which is due to \cref{eq:6}. Therefore, we can estimate
  \begin{align*}
    \abs{e_{n_k^-}(x,y)(\phi) &- \delta_-(\phi)}\\
    &=
    \abs[\Bigg]{
    \frac{1}{n_k^-}\sum_{i = 0}^{n_k^- - 1} \phi(F^i(x,y))
      -
      \int_X \phi(z,0) \;d\mu(z)
      }\\
    &\leq
      \abs[\Bigg]{
      \frac{1}{n_k^-}\sum_{i = 0}^{n_k^- - 1} \phi(F^i(x,y))
      -
        \frac{1}{n_k^-}\sum_{i = 0}^{n_k^- - 1} \phi(F^i(x,0))
      } + \varepsilon
    \\
    &\le
      \frac{1}{n^-_k}
      \sum_{\substack{0 \leq i < n_k^- \\[1pt] i \in D}}
    \abs*{\phi(F^i(x,y)) - \phi(F^i(x,0))}
    +  \frac{2}{n^-_k}\sum_{\substack{0 \leq i < n^-_k \\[1pt] i \notin D}} \lVert \phi \rVert_{\infty} + \varepsilon\\
    &\le
      \frac{1}{n^-_k}
      \sum_{\substack{0 \leq i < n_k^- \\[1pt] i \in D}}
    \abs*{\phi(F^i(x,y)) - \phi(F^i(x,0))} + 3\varepsilon\\
    &\leq \varepsilon + 3\varepsilon = 4\varepsilon.
  \end{align*}
  This completes the proof.
\end{proof}


\begin{corollary}\label{col:histo-avoid-compacts}
  If, in addition to the hypotheses of \cref{thm:wip-historical}, for any compact \(K \subseteq (0,1)\) the average visiting time of \(f^n_x(y)\) to \(K\) tends to zero as \(n \to \infty\) for almost every \(x\) for some (and therefore, for any) \(y \in (0,1)\), that is,
  \begin{equation*}
    \frac{1}{n}\#\set{ 0 \le i < n \where f^i_x(y) \in K} \to 0,
  \end{equation*}
  then
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:4}
    \set{e_n(x,y)}[n \in \NN, '] = [\delta_-, \delta_+]
  \end{equation}
  for \(\mu\)-almost every \(x\) and all \(y \in (0,1)\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
  Let \(E \subseteq X\) be a full measure subset of the basin of \(\mu\) that satisfies both the hypothesis and the result of \cref{thm:wip-historical}. For \(x \in E\) and \(y \in (0,1)\), let \(\Emp[n_k]{x,y} \weakto \eta\) be any limit point of the empirical measures. First, we show \(\supp \eta \subseteq X \times \set{0,1}\). Indeed, let \(x_0 \in X\) and \(y_0 \in (0,1)\), and let \(\U\) be a neighborhood of \((x_0,y_0)\) such that \(\U[closure] \subseteq X \times (0,1)\). Since \(\U[closure]\) is compact and \(x \in E\), by hypothesis, \(\Emp[n_k]{x,y}(\U) \to 0\), so by \nameref{thm:portmanteau}
  \begin{equation*}
    \eta(\U) \leq \liminf_{k \to \infty} \Emp[n_k]{x,y}(\U) = 0.
  \end{equation*}
  This shows \((x_0,y_0) \notin \supp \eta\), so \(\supp \eta \subseteq X \times \set{0,1}\).

  We know \(\eta\) is a limiting measure for \(x\), so it is invariant by \(F\). The fact \(x\) is in the basin of \(\mu\) implies by definition that \((\pi_1)_* \eta = \mu\). This means that \(\eta \ll \delta_- + \delta_+\), because for all measurable \(A\),
  \[\eta(A) = \eta(A \cap X \times \set{0,1})\le \mu(\set{x \where (x,0) \in A}) + \mu(\set{x \where (x,1) \in A}) = (\delta_- + \delta_+)(A).\]
  Since \(\delta_{ - }\) and \(\delta_+\) are ergodic measures, \(\eta\) must be a convex combination of the two.

  We have shown \(\Meas{x,y} \subseteq [\delta_-,\delta_+]\). To conclude \cref{eq:emp-closure}, it suffices to notice that by \cref{prop:limiting-measures}, \(\Meas{x,y}\) is connected, and therefore, since by \cref{thm:wip-historical} it contains both endpoints \(\delta_-\) and \(\delta_+\), it must be the whole segment.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Almost sure invariance principle}

Alternatively, all the hypotheses of both \cref{thm:wip-historical,col:histo-avoid-compacts} are satisfied when the pair \((g,p)\) satisfies an \emph{almost sure} invariance principle. By that, we mean there exists \(\sigma > 0\) and \(\delta \in (0,1/2)\) and new probability space \((\Omega,\Prob)\), along with two sequences of random variables \(\zeta = (\zeta_n)_n\) and \(\beta = (\beta_n)_n\), where \(\zeta_n, \beta_n \colon \Omega \to \RR\), such that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \label{item:1}\(\zeta\) and \(\xi = (p \circ g^n)_n\) have the same distribution: \(\zeta_*\Prob = \xi_*\mu \in \Meas[1]{\RR^{\NN}}\);
\item \(\beta_n\) are independent and identically distributed as \(\Normal[0][\sigma]\);
\item \label{item:3}we can match the partial sums \(\PSum[n,rv = \zeta]\) and \(\PSum[n,rv = \beta]\), in the sense that for \(\Prob\)-almost every \(\omega \in \Omega\),
  \begin{equation*}
    \frac{\PSum[n,rv = \zeta]{\omega} - \PSum[n,rv = \beta]{\omega}}{n^{\delta}} \to 0.
  \end{equation*}
\end{enumerate}
In particular, since \(\DProc[n,rv = \beta,spar,pf]\Prob \weakto \sigma W\) by \cref{thm:donsker}, this condition implies that \(\DProc[n,rv = \xi,spar,pf]\mu = \DProc[n,rv = \zeta,spar,pf]\Prob\) converges to the same limit \(\sigma W\).

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:avoid-compacts-asip}
  When the pair \((g,p)\) satisfies an almost sure invariance principle, the extra condition of \cref{col:histo-avoid-compacts} is satisfied.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  Fixing a compact \(K \subseteq \RR\), \cref{item:1} implies that\sidetodo{new :)}
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:9}
    \mu\rpar[\bigg]{\set[par = \Big]{x \in X \where \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i = 0}^{n - 1}1_K(\PSum[i](x)) \to 0}}
    =
    \Prob\rpar[\bigg]{\set[par = \Big]{\omega \in \Omega \where \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i = 0}^{n - 1}1_K(\PSum[i,rv = \zeta](\omega)) \to 0}}.
  \end{equation}
  By a change of coordinates using \(\gamma\), it suffices to show the right-hand side of \cref{eq:9} is equal to \(1\). By choosing \(M > 0\) big enough, it is also sufficient to consider \(K = [-M,M]\).

  Define
  \[\Omega_C = \set[par = \bigg]{\omega \in \Omega \where \sup_n \frac{\abs{\PSum[n,rv = \zeta]{\omega} - \PSum[n,rv = \beta]{\omega}}}{n^{\delta}} \le C}.\]
  Notice that by \cref{item:3}, \(\Omega = \bigcup_{C = 1}^{\infty} \Omega_C\).

  Fix \(C\), and let \(\Prob_C(A) = \Prob(A \cap \Omega_C)\). We estimate the rate of decay of \(p_n = \Prob[C]{\abs{\PSum[n,rv = \zeta]} \le M}\). Let \(N_C\) be big enough that \(n \ge N_C\) implies \(M \le Cn^{\delta}\). By the triangle inequality, we have \(p_n \le \Prob[C](\abs{\PSum[n,rv = \beta]} \le 2Cn^{\delta})\) for \(n \ge N_C\). Since \(\PSum[n,rv = \beta] \sim \Normal[0][n \sigma]\),
  \begin{equation*}
    \Prob[C](\abs{\PSum[n,rv = \beta]} \le 2Cn^{\delta}) \le \frac{4Cn^{\delta}}{\sqrt{2\pi n \sigma}} = O(n^{\delta - 1/2}).
  \end{equation*}
  Therefore, \(p_n = O(n^{\delta - 1/2})\). Now, let
  \[A_n(\omega) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i = 0}^{n - 1} 1_K(\PSum[i,rv = \zeta]{\omega}), \]
  and let \(a_n = \CE[\Prob[C]]{A_n} = \int_{\Omega_C} A_n \;d\Prob\). By Fubini,
  \(a_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i = 0}^{n - 1} p_i\), and since \(\delta - 1/2 \in (-1/2,0)\), we also have \(a_n = O(n^{\delta - 1/2})\).


  Let \(n_k = 2^k\), \(\varepsilon > 0\) and \(E_k = \set{\omega \in \Omega_C \where A_{n_k}(\omega) > \varepsilon}\). By Markov's inequality, \(\Prob(E_k) \le a_{n_k}/\varepsilon\).
  Since \(\delta - 1/2 < 0\), we therefore have \(\sum_{k = 0}^{\infty} \Prob(E_k) < \infty\). Applying Borel-Cantelli, \[\Prob(\limsup E_k) = \Prob(\set{\omega \in \Omega_C\where \limsup_{k \to \infty} A_{n_k}(\omega) > \varepsilon}) = 0.\]
  Since \(\varepsilon > 0\) and \(C > 0\) are arbitrary, this shows \(\lim_{k \to \infty} A_{n_k} = 0\) almost surely. To recover the same limit for the original sequence, we simply notice that \(1_K \ge 0\), so
  \begin{equation*}
    A_n \le A_{n_{k + 1}} \cdot \frac{n_{k + 1}}{n_k} = 2 A_{n_{k + 1}}
  \end{equation*}
  and therefore \(A_n \to 0\) almost surely.
\end{proof}

  The almost sure invariance principle (ASIP) holds for a large class of dynamics and observables. Here, we highlight the Theorem 2.1 of~\cite{korepanovRatesAlmostSure2018}, where it is shown that the ASIP holds for every \(\delta > 0\) when \(g\) is a nonuniformly expanding map and \(p\) is a Hölder continuous function with \(\int p \;d\mu = 0\). This includes the original example of \citeauthor{bonifantSchwarzianDerivativesCylinder2008} in which \(g(x) = kx \pmod 1\).

\section{Deriving the weak invariance principle}\label{sec:deriv-weak-invar}

The following proof is adapted from the argument at the end of the proof of~\cite[Theorem 19.1]{billingsleyConvergenceProbabilityMeasures1999}.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:martingale-coboundary}
  Suppose \(p \in L^2(\mu)\) can be decomposed as a sum
  \begin{equation}
    p = q + s \circ g - s
  \end{equation}
  such that \(0 < \norm{q}_2 < \infty\) and \(\zeta = (q \circ g^i)_{i \geq 0}\) is a reversed martingale difference sequence. Then, \(\xi = (p \circ g^i)_{i \ge 0}\) is such that \(\DProc[n,spar,pf]\mu \weakto \sigma W\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
  Notice that
\begin{equation*}
  \PSum[n,rv = \xi] = \PSum[n,rv = \zeta] + s \circ g^n - s,
\end{equation*}
so
\begin{equation*}
  \DProc[n,rv=\xi]{x}{t}
  = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\PSum[\floor{nt},rv=\xi]{x} = \DProc[n,rv=\zeta]{x}{t} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \big(s \circ g^{\floor{nt}} - s\big)(x).
\end{equation*}
By \cref{cor:donsker-martingale}, \(\DProc[n,rv = \zeta,spar,pf]\mu \weakto \sigma W\), so it suffices to show that for \(\mu\)-almost every \(x\), the second term in the sum converges uniformly to zero (as a function of \(t\)). Given \(\varepsilon > 0\), we look at the sets \(B_n = \{ x :s(g^n(x))^2\geq n \varepsilon \}\). Since
\begin{equation*}
  \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty}\mu(B_n) = \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty}\mu(\set{s^2 \geq n \varepsilon}) = \int \floor{s^2/ \varepsilon} \;d\mu \leq \frac{1}{\varepsilon} \lVert s \rVert_2^2,
\end{equation*}
by Borel-Cantelli, \(\mu(\limsup_{n \to \infty}B_n) = 0\). If we take the intersection over \(k\) of the sets \(\liminf_{n \to \infty}B_n^{\complement}\) for \(\varepsilon = 1/k\), we get a full-measure set \(G\) with the property that for every \(x \in G\) and \(\varepsilon > 0\), there exists \(N\) such that \(n \geq N\) implies \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}|s(g^n(x))| \leq \varepsilon\). Now, let \(M \geq N\) be big enough so that \(n \geq M\) implies \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}|s(g^i(x))| \leq \varepsilon\) for all \(0 \leq i \leq N\). So for \(n \geq M\),
\begin{equation*}
  \sup_{t \in [0,1]}\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \big\lvert s \circ g^{\floor{nt}} - s\big\rvert(x) \leq 
  \max_{0 \leq i \leq N}\frac{|s \circ g^{i}(x)|}{\sqrt{n}} + 
  \max_{N \leq i \le n}\frac{|s \circ g^{i}(x)|}{\sqrt{i}} 
  \leq 2\varepsilon.
\end{equation*}
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
  If \(p\) was a coboundary \(p = \phi \circ g - \phi\) to begin with (that is, \(\norm{q}_2 = 0\)), then it is clear that \(\PSum[n,rv = \xi] = \phi \circ g^n - \phi\), so the iterates of the skew product \(F(x,y) = (g(x), y + p(x))\) satisfy
  \begin{equation*}
    F^n(x_0,y_0) = (g^n(x_0), \phi(g^n(x_0)) - \phi(x_0) + y_0) \subseteq \graph(\psi)
  \end{equation*}
  and are thus contained in the graph of \(\psi\), where \(\psi(x) = \phi(x) - \phi(x_0) + y_0\).
\end{remark}

We will call the decomposition in \cref{thm:martingale-coboundary} a reverse martingale-coboundary decomposition. In the rest of the section, we derive conditions for it to exist in two different situations: non-invertible, and invertible \(g\).

\subsection{Non-invertible case}
Contrary to the invertible case, when \(g\) is not invertible, the sub-\(\sigma\)-algebras \(\mathcal{F}_j = g^{-j}(\mathcal{F})\) form a strictly decreasing sequence in general. We will follow~\cite[Proposition 2.12]{vianaStochasticDynamicsDeterministic2021} for the next theorem.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:decomposition-existence}
  Suppose \(p \in L^2(\mu)\) and \(\mathcal{F}_j = g^{-n}(\mathcal{F})\) are such that
  \begin{equation*}
    \sum_{n = 0}^{\infty} \lVert \CE[\mu]{p, \mathcal{F}_j} \rVert_2 < \infty.
  \end{equation*}
  Then a reverse martingale-coboundary decomposition exists. Moreover, this implies that
  \begin{equation*}
    \int p \;d\mu = \int q \;d\mu = 0
  \end{equation*}
  and we have the equality
  \begin{equation*}
    \sigma ^2 = \lVert q \rVert^2_2 = \lVert p \rVert_2^2 + 2\sum_{n = 1}^{\infty} \int p \cdot p \circ g^n \;d\mu.
  \end{equation*}
  This gives a way to calculate \(\sigma\) in terms of \(p\). By \cref{thm:martingale-coboundary}, if \(\sigma > 0\), then \(\DProc[n,rv = \xi,spar,pf]\mu \weakto \sigma W\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
  \newcommand{\pp}{\phi^+}
  \newcommand{\pn}{\phi^-}
  Denote by \(U \colon L^2(\mu) \to L^2(\mu)\) the Koopman operator \(U(\varphi) = \varphi \circ g\). Let \(L \colon L^2(\mu) \to L^2(\mu)\) be its adjoint:
  \begin{equation*}
    \int L(\varphi)\cdot \psi \;d\mu = \int \varphi \cdot U(\psi) \;d\mu.
  \end{equation*}
  Since \(U\) is an isometry into \(U(L^2(\mu)) = L^2(\mathcal{F}_1)\), the restriction \(L \mid L^2(\mathcal{F}_1)\) is an isometry as well. Moreover, we have \(UL\mid  L^2(\mathcal{F}_1) = \Id \mid L^2(\mathcal{F}_1)\) (but be careful: \(\ker L = L^2(\mathcal{F}_1)^{\bot}\)).

  
  Define
  \begin{equation*}
    s = \sum_{i = 1}^{\infty} L^i(\CE[\mu]{p,\mathcal{F}_i})\quad\text{ and }\quad
    q = \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} L^i(\CE[\mu]{p,\mathcal{F}_i} - \CE[\mu]{p, \mathcal{F}_{i + 1}}),
  \end{equation*}
  whose series converge due to the hypothesis and \(L\) being an isometry. Rewriting,
  \begin{align*}
    q &=
        \CE[\mu]{p,\mathcal{F}_0} + 
        \sum_{i = 1}^{\infty} L^i(\CE[\mu]{p,\mathcal{F}_i}) - 
        \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} L^i(\CE[\mu]{p, \mathcal{F}_{i + 1}})\\
      &=
        p +
        \sum_{i = 1}^{\infty} L^i(\CE[\mu]{p,\mathcal{F}_i}) - 
        \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} UL(L^i(\CE[\mu]{p, \mathcal{F}_{i + 1}}))\\
      &=
        p +
        \sum_{i = 1}^{\infty} L^i(\CE[\mu]{p,\mathcal{F}_i}) - 
        \sum_{i = 1}^{\infty} L^i(\CE[\mu]{p, \mathcal{F}_{i}}) \circ g\\
      &=
        p + s - s \circ g.
  \end{align*}
  Therefore \(p = q + s \circ g - s\). We now check \(q\) is a reverse martingale. For that, notice that \(L^i(L^2(\mathcal{F}_{i + 1})^{\bot}) = L^2(\mathcal{F}_1)^{\bot}\), and since \(\mathcal{F}_{i + 1} \subseteq \mathcal{F}_i\),
  \[
    \int_A\CE[\mu]{p,\mathcal{F}_i} - \CE[\mu]{p, \mathcal{F}_{i + 1}} \;d\mu =
    \int_A p \;d\mu - \int_A p \;d\mu =
    0,
  \]
  for all \(A \in \mathcal{F}_{i + 1}\), so \(\CE[\mu]{p,\mathcal{F}_i} - \CE[\mu]{p, \mathcal{F}_{i + 1}} \in L^2(\mathcal{F}_{i + 1})^{\bot}\). Then, \(q \in L^2(\mathcal{F}_1)^{\bot}\), so \(\CE[\mu]{q,\mathcal{F}_1} = 0\) and \(\xi\) is an stationary ergodic reverse martingale difference sequence. It's also clear that
  \begin{equation*}
    \int p \;d\mu = \int q + s \circ g - s \;d\mu = \int q \;d\mu = \int \CE[\mu]{q,\mathcal{F}_1} \;d\mu = 0.
  \end{equation*}
  Also, for any \(0 \le i < j\),
  \begin{equation*}
    \int (q \circ g^i)(q \circ g^j) \;d\mu = 
    \int q \cdot (q \circ g^j) \;d\mu = 0
  \end{equation*}
  because \(q \circ g^j \in L^2(\mathcal{F}_j) \subseteq L^2(\mathcal{F}_1)\) and \(q \in L^2(\mathcal{F}_1)^{\bot}\).
  Therefore,
  \begin{equation*}
    \norm{q}_2^2
    = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i = 0}^{n - 1} \norm{q \circ g^i}_2^2
    = \norm[\Big]{\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum_{i = 0}^{n - 1} q \circ g^i}_2^2.
  \end{equation*}
  We also have
  \begin{equation*}
    \norm[\Big]{\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum_{i = 0}^{n - 1} p \circ g^i - \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum_{i = 0}^{n - 1} q \circ g^i}_2
    = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\norm{ s \circ g^{n} - s}_2
    \le \frac{2\norm{s}_2}{\sqrt{n}} \to 0,
  \end{equation*}
  so
  \begin{align*}
    \norm{q}_2^2
    &= \lim_{n \to \infty} 
    \norm[\Big]{\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum_{i = 0}^{n - 1} p \circ g^i}_2^2\\
    &= \lim_{n \to \infty} 
    \frac{1}{n}\rpar[\Big]{\sum_{i = 0}^{n - 1} \norm{p \circ g^i}_2^2
      + 2 \sum_{0 \le i < j < n} \int (p \circ g^i)(p \circ g^j) \;d\mu
    }\\
    &= \norm{p}_2^2 +
      2\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n}
      \sum_{0 \le i < j < n} \int p \cdot (p \circ g^{j - i}) \;d\mu
      .\stepcounter{equation}\tag{\theequation}\label{normeq}
  \end{align*}
  \begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=150pt]{../figures/gridsum}
    \caption{Depiction of the identity \(\sum_{0 \le i < j < n} f(j - i) = \sum_{i = 1}^n (n - i)f(i)\).}\label{fig:sum-identity}
    % \caption{The sum of \(f(j - i)\) over \(0 \le i < j < n\) equals the sum of \((n - i)f(i)\) over \(1 \le i \le n\).}
  \end{figure}
  Now, we have (see \cref{fig:sum-identity})
  \begin{equation*}
    \sum_{0 \le i < j < n} \int p \cdot (p \circ g^{j - i}) \;d\mu
    = 
    \sum_{i = 1}^{n}(n - i) \int p \cdot (p \circ g^{i}) \;d\mu.
  \end{equation*}
  Since \(p \circ g^i \in L^2(\mathcal{F}_i)\), by Cauchy-Schwarz,
  \begin{equation*}
    \int p \cdot (p \circ g^{i}) \;d\mu = 
    \int \CE[\mu]{p,\mathcal{F}_i} (p \circ g^{i}) \;d\mu
    \le \norm{p}_2 \norm{\CE[\mu]{p,\mathcal{F}_i}}_2
  \end{equation*}
  and therefore
  \begin{equation*}
    \sum_{i = 1}^{\infty} \int p \cdot (p \circ g^{i}) \;d\mu < \infty
  \end{equation*}
  by the assumption. This implies \(i \int p \cdot (p \circ g^{i}) \;d\mu \to 0\), so the Cesàro average
  \begin{equation*}
    \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i = 1}^{n}i \int p\cdot(p\circ g^{i}) \;d\mu
    \to 0
  \end{equation*}
  converges to the same limit. Substituting in \cref{normeq},
  \begin{equation*}
    \norm{q}_2^2 = \norm{p}_2^2 + 2\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i = 1}^{n} n \int p\cdot(p\circ g^{i}) \;d\mu
    = \norm{p}_2^2 + 2\sum_{i = 1}^{\infty}\int p\cdot(p\circ g^{i}) \;d\mu.
  \end{equation*}
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}\label{cor:decay-implies-summable}
  Suppose \(\mathcal{G} \subseteq \mathcal{F}\) is a sub-\(\sigma\)-algebra and \(p\) is such that there exists a summable sequence \(c_n > 0\) satisfying
  \begin{equation*}
    \int p \cdot (\psi \circ g^n) \;d\mu \leq \lVert \psi \rVert_2c_n
  \end{equation*}
  for all \(\psi \in L^2(\mathcal{G})\). Then
  \begin{equation*}
    \sum_{n = 0}^{\infty} \lVert \CE{p, g^{-n}(\mathcal{G})} \rVert_2 < \infty.
  \end{equation*}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
  Since \(\CE{\cdot, \mathcal{A}}\) is the orthogonal projection onto \(L^2(\mathcal{A})\),
  \begin{align*}
    \CE{p, g^{-n}(\mathcal{G})}
    &= \sup \set[par = \Big]{\int p \cdot \xi \;d\mu \where \xi \in L^2(g^{-n}(\mathcal{G})),\;\norm{\xi}_2 = 1}\\
    &= \sup \set[par = \Big]{\int p \cdot (\psi \circ g^n) \;d\mu \where \psi \in L^2(\mathcal{G}),\;\norm{\psi}_2 = 1}\\
    &\le \norm{\psi}_2 c_n.
  \end{align*}
\end{proof}

\subsection{Invertible case}

If \(g\) is invertible (with measurable inverse), then \(\mathcal{F} = g^{-1}(\mathcal{F})\), so there is no hope of applying \cref{thm:decomposition-existence}. Still, in this case, we may be able to find a smaller \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\mathcal{G} \subseteq \mathcal{F}\) for which \(g^{-1}\) ``forgets'' about the past values of \(p\) fast enough, but \(\CE{p,g^n(\mathcal{G})}\) is still asymptotically close to \(p\).
For some intuition, suppose \(X = \set{0,1}^{\ZZ}\) and \(g\) was the shift \(g((x_i)) = x_{i + 1}\). If we let \(\mathcal{G} \subseteq \mathcal{F}\) be the \(\sigma\)-algebra generated by the projections \(\pi_j\) on positive coordinates \(j \ge 0\), then for a continuous \(p\), we expect that \(\CE[\mu]{p,g^{-n}(\mathcal{G})} \to \int p \;d\mu\) for this smaller algebra (because \(\bigcap_{n = 1}^{\infty} g^{-n}(\mathcal{G}) = \{\varnothing, X\}\)), but we expect \(\CE[\mu]{p,g^{n}(\mathcal{G})} \to p\) (because \(\bigcup_{n = 1}^{\infty} g^{n}(\mathcal{G}) = \mathcal{F}\)). This property will let us recover the main ingredients used the proof of \cref{thm:decomposition-existence}.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:decomposition-existence-inv}
  Suppose \(p \in L^2(\mu)\), the inverse of \(g\) is measurable and there is a smaller \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\mathcal{G} \subseteq \mathcal{F}\) for which \(\mathcal{G}_j = g^{-j}(\mathcal{G})\) satisfies
  \begin{equation*}
    \sum_{n = 0}^{\infty} \lVert \CE[\mu]{p, \mathcal{G}_n} \rVert_2 < \infty\quad \text{ and }
    \quad \sum_{n = 0}^{\infty} \norm{p - \CE[\mu]{p,\mathcal{G}_{-n}}}_2 < \infty.
  \end{equation*}
  Then a reverse martingale-coboundary decomposition \(p = q + s \circ g - s\) for \(q \in L^2(\mathcal{G})\) exists and the same conclusions of \cref{thm:decomposition-existence} hold.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
  \NewObject\Notation{\OP}{\mathbb{P}}[
    output=\RandomVariable,
    set arg sep={\where}
  ]
  Let \(U,L \colon L^2(\mu) \to L^2(\mu)\) be adjoint to each other as before:
  \[
    \begin{tikzcd}
      {L^2(\mathcal{G}_i)} & {L^2(\mathcal{G}_{i+1})}
      \arrow["U", bend left, from=1-1, to=1-2]
      \arrow["L", bend left, from=1-2, to=1-1].
    \end{tikzcd}
  \]
  We introduce a new operator \(\OP[\mu]{\phi,\mathcal{A}} = \phi - \CE[\mu]{\phi,\mathcal{A}}\), which on \(L^2(\mu)\) is precisely the orthogonal projection onto \(L^2(\mathcal{A})^{\bot}\).

  The proof strategy will be as follows: we will decompose \(p = p^+ + p^-\) under the splitting \(L^2(\mu) = L^2(\mathcal{G}) \oplus L^2(\mathcal{G})^{\bot}\), that is, \(p^+ = \CE[\mu]{p,\mathcal{G}}\) and \(p^- = \OP[\mu]{p,\mathcal{G}}\). The \(p^+\) part will work just as in \cref{thm:decomposition-existence}, while for \(p^-\), a dual argument will apply, where we exchange \(L\) for \(U\).
  
  Therefore, define
  \begin{equation*}
    s^+ = \sum_{i = 1}^{\infty} L^i(\CE[\mu]{p,\mathcal{G}_i})\quad\text{ and }\quad
    q^+ = \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} L^i(\CE[\mu]{p,\mathcal{G}_i} - \CE[\mu]{p, \mathcal{G}_{i + 1}}),
  \end{equation*}
  \begin{equation*}
    s^- = - \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} U^i(\OP[\mu]{p,\mathcal{G}_{-i}})\quad\text{ and }\quad
    q^- = \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} U^i(\OP[\mu]{p,\mathcal{G}_{-i + 1}} - \OP[\mu]{p, \mathcal{G}_{-i}}).
  \end{equation*}
  The series are absolutely convergent by the hypotheses. Now, already from the definitions, both \(q^+\) and \(q^-\) are martingale difference sequences. Indeed,
  \begin{align*}
    &\OP[\mu]{p,\mathcal{G}_{-i + 1}} - \OP[\mu]{p, \mathcal{G}_{-i}}
      = \CE[\mu]{p,\mathcal{G}_{-i}} - \CE[\mu]{p,\mathcal{G}_{-i + 1}}
      \in L^2(\mathcal{G}_i) \ominus L^2(\mathcal{G}_{-i + 1})\\
    &\qquad\implies
    U^i(\OP[\mu]{p,\mathcal{G}_{-i + 1}} - \OP[\mu]{p, \mathcal{G}_{-i}})
      \in L^2(\mathcal{G}) \ominus L^2(\mathcal{G}_{1})\\
    &\qquad\implies
    q^-
      \in L^2(\mathcal{G}) \ominus L^2(\mathcal{G}_{1}),
  \end{align*}
  and similarly, \(q^+ \in L^2(\mathcal{G}) \ominus L^2(\mathcal{G}_{1})\).

  Moreover, we have
  \begin{align*}
    q^+ &=
        \CE[\mu]{p,\mathcal{G}_0} + 
        \sum_{i = 1}^{\infty} L^i(\CE[\mu]{p,\mathcal{G}_i}) - 
        \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} L^i(\CE[\mu]{p, \mathcal{G}_{i + 1}})\\
      &=
        p^+ +
        \sum_{i = 1}^{\infty} L^i(\CE[\mu]{p,\mathcal{G}_i}) - 
        \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} UL(L^i(\CE[\mu]{p, \mathcal{G}_{i + 1}}))\\
      &=
        p^+ +
        \sum_{i = 1}^{\infty} L^i(\CE[\mu]{p,\mathcal{G}_i}) - 
        \sum_{i = 1}^{\infty} L^i(\CE[\mu]{p, \mathcal{G}_{i}}) \circ g\\
      &=
        p^+ + s^+ - s^+ \circ g
  \end{align*}
  and
  \begin{align*}
    q^- &=
        \OP[\mu]{p,\mathcal{G}_0} + 
        \sum_{i = 1}^{\infty} U^i(\OP[\mu]{p,\mathcal{G}_{-i + 1}}) - 
        \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} U^i(\OP[\mu]{p, \mathcal{G}_{-i}})\\
      &=
        p^- +
        \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} U(U^i(\OP[\mu]{p,\mathcal{G}_{-i}})) - 
        \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} U^i(\OP[\mu]{p, \mathcal{G}_{-i}})\\
      &=
        p^- +
        \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} U^i(\OP[\mu]{p,\mathcal{G}_{-i}}) \circ g - 
        \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} U^i(\OP[\mu]{p, \mathcal{G}_{-i}})\\
      &=
        p^- + s^- - s^- \circ g.
  \end{align*}
  Therefore \(p = q + s \circ g - s\) for \(q = q^+ + q^-\) and \(s = s^+ + s^-\). This completes the necessary ingredients for \cref{thm:decomposition-existence}.
\end{proof}
